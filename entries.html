Mapping Black Rome

Via Margutta

William Demby

I was bent on going to Rome from day one! [...] Rome was for the gritty life of the pure artist as depicted in Roberto Rossellini’s films (Micconi 126.)

So convincing was the circle around me and my sudden immersion and acceptance as an artist into Europe and its revolutionary post-war culture that Beetlecreek almost seemed to write itself (Micconi 127).

William Demby was stationed in Italy during World War II. After graduating from Fisk University after the war, he returned to Italy in 1947 and worked as a novelist, actor, journalist, screenwriter, and translator of Italian film scripts [1]. In 1953, he married Italian poet, novelist, translator, and literary agent Lucia Drudi, with whom he would go on to co-author screenplays including Congo vivo and Marcia o crepa in the early 1960s [2].

Via Margutta, where Demby first lived in Rome upon his return in 1947, is a lane off the Piazza del Popolo that hosted many artists’ studios and living spaces. Not long after, Demby would move into a shared flat with several artists including Renzo Vespignani and Marcello Muccini on Via San Teodoro overlooking the ruins of the Ancient City where he would continue writing Beetlecreek.

Edmonia Lewis

In the 19th century, American artists were drawn to the area near the Spanish Steps, notably African American and Native American sculptor Edmonia Lewis, who had a studio on the Via Margutta in the former studio of the renowned Italian sculptor Canova (1757-1822). Lewis’s work showcased African American and Native American women, often in marble, once she moved to Rome. Specifically, she highlighted the oppression of Black people and celebrated her own Native American heritage. Lewis did her own stonework to ensure it was accepted as her original work, rather than following the standard practice of hiring workmen to carve the final piece [3].

Piazza Del Popolo

William Demby

Today is yesterday and T., Alice and I are seated at Rosati’s café in Piazza del Popolo waiting for P. [Pier Paolo Pasonlini], the movie director. Alice hopes to get a small part in P.’s new film, La Mamma. While we are waiting for P. to come I let Alice read the first chapter of this novel. (Demby, The Catacombs 30).

Writer William Demby forged a multifaceted career working in postwar Rome as a novelist, screenwriter, script translator, actor, and journalist [1]. He alludes to his experiences in his semi-autobiographical novel The Catacombs (1965), whose protagonist, Bill Demby, is a Black author living in Rome in the early 1960s with his Italian wife and son and writing a novel within the novel about a Black actress, Doris. In the postwar period, the Piazza del Popolo and surrounding streets stretching to the Spanish Steps, were filmmaker haunts, including cafes like Rosati and Canova. Nearby, the Via Margutta was lined with artist studios.

Villa Borghese

Ben Johnson

Ben Johnson was a prolific African American translator of Italian modern literature into English, actor, editor, and writer who lived in postwar Rome [1]. Before settling in Rome, he served in Italy during WWII, and then returned to the United States to earn his degree at Fisk University in Nashville, Tennessee. There, he met writer William Demby and stayed in close contact with him when he moved to Rome in the late 1940s. Johnson translated into English many groundbreaking modern and contemporary Italian literary texts, including works by Italo Svevo; Johnson was Italo Calvino’s first translator into English. Johnson worked as an editor on the cutting-edge, Rome-based journal Botteghe Oscure.

Johnson starred in a 1952 retelling of Othello set in Rome, Anna’s Sin (Il peccato di Anna) [2], in which his friend writer William Demby also played a substantive role. The film features the Villa Borghese gallery in one of its key scenes. The Villa Borghese gallery is one of the most famous art galleries in the world, containing paintings, sculptures and antiquities.

Botteghe Oscure

Ben Johnson

Ben Johnson worked on the cutting-edge journal Botteghe Oscure as an editor, translator, and proofreader, along with editor Giorgio Bassani and Anglo-American expatriate writer Eugene Walter. American expatriate Marguerite Caetani backed the journal and housed its production in her residence the Palazzo Caetani in the center of Rome and at her estate at Ninfa [1].

Johnson was the chief editor (redattore) for volume fourteen of the journal (1954), which includes a short story by William Demby, who published his work in multiple issues of the journal. Between 1948–1960, Botteghe Oscure published cutting-edge writing twice a year from Rome and was remarkable for its global distribution and for publishing authors in their original languages.

Folkstudio

Harold Bradley

Originally from Chicago, Harold Bradley moved to Italy in 1959 to study at the University for Foreigners in Perugia, Italy. While in Italy, he founded the cultural hub Folkstudio in 1961 in Rome’s Trastevere neighborhood, with art exhibitions in the day that morphed into a jazz club by night, frequented by artists and musicians including Bob Dylan, Pete Seeger, and Ravi Shankar. Bradley was accomplished in a multitude of different fields. He starred in over a dozen Italian films, even making appearances as an extra in the film Cleopatra with Elizabeth Taylor. He was a painter, musician, educator, art historian, film, theater actor, football player as well as a constant patron for African American influence in the arts. After moving back to the United States in 1968, he taught at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign and produced and hosted three prime-time public affairs shows where he focused on race and intercultural issues [1].

American Academy in Rome

Barbara Chase-Riboud

When we landed in Rome, the effect was startling. Everything in Paris is this sort of filtered gray light. In Rome everything is golden. (Chase-Riboud, I Always Knew 11)

The [American] Academy itself is unbelievable. It’s just outside of Rome, in a very quiet residential section of huge villas and gardens. . . t here are big marble steps leading through the facade of the building which is a series of big arched windows into the most beautiful inside court with big poplar trees, gardens and a romantic-looking fountain in the middle. … I wish I could stay here and then I don’t. It would be great for work but it’s so isolated and so American. (Chase-Riboud, I Always Knew 11)

Sculptor and writer Barbara Chase-Riboud began writing poetry and creating art at an early age in Philadelphia, PA. After studying at Temple University’s Tyler School of Art, she was awarded a John Hay Whitney fellowship in 1957 housed at the American Academy in Rome. It was in Italy where she developed her now iconic wax-casting sculptural technique [1]. She sought out her own living and work space during her time in Italy and made a solo trip to Egypt that forever changed her approach to her work. The artist continues to this day to divide her time between Rome and Paris [2].

John Rhoden

John Rhoden was born March 13, 1916, and showed artistic promise from a young age. He went on to join the military and during his time serving he completed the busts of several high-ranking officers. After the military, he attended the New School for Social Research and then Columbia University which eventually led him to his time at the American Academy in Rome in 1951 to 1954. While in Rome, Rhoden, the first Black visual artist to be in residence at the Academy, created a towering bronze sculpture whose “lithe organicism and integration of figuration and abstraction presaged the style that would characterize his art for years to come” [3].

Rhoden’s time in Rome uniquely shaped his artistic style and caught the attention of the United States Department of State who recruited him to serve as an art specialist from 1955 through 1959. His time abroad, and especially in Rome, established Rhoden as a prominent sculptor of the time and allowed for exploration of a multitude of cultures and artistic representations.

Ralph Ellison

My wife and I were living the dormitory life of the American Academy in Rome and I was much preoccupied: with writing a novel, with literary concepts, with reviewing my own life’s resources for literary creation; with the experience of discovering a foreign culture in which the old structures and guides that sustained my thoughts, emotions and conduct at home were so relaxed, whether awake or sleeping, that it was as though I was living in a barely controlled chaos. (Ellison, “Tell It Like It Is, Baby”)

Writer Ralph Ellison spent two years as a Rome Prize Fellow in Literature at the American Academy in Rome from 1955-1957, following the publication of his first, critically acclaimed novel Invisible Man. There, he would begin drafting his second novel, which was to remain forever in progress, but was published posthumously in two edited variants, Juneteenth and Three Days Before the Shooting [4].

Seemingly disillusioned with the Eternal City, Ellison observed in a letter sent from Rome to his best friend Albert Murray that “the best of Rome remains its past” [5].

RAI Studios: episode of Il signore delle 21: “Harlem”

William Demby

Today is May 12, [1962], Doris and the Count are here to watch the TV program Il Signore delle 21: Harlem, in which I appear as a kind of assistant master of ceremonies, together with Louis Armstrong, Sammy Davis, Hazel Scott, the Peters Sisters and many other stars of the Negro entertainment world. I know that this is the first time that so many Negro entertainers have appeared at one time on a European television program and I am frankly apprehensive. Strange things happened during the Ampex recording of the program. For years I had lived under the illusion that racial prejudice does not exist in Italy, which has now become my second patria—And yet—had I been imagining things? (Demby, The Catacombs 49-50)

Writer William Demby was asked to MC an episode of the Italian variety show Il signore delle 21, an experience he would fold into his semi-autobiographical novel The Catacombs. Demby’s idea for the episode was “to say, ‘Hey Louis, what’s Harlem?’ And I told him that, when I asked that, he should say, ‘Harlem is…,’ and then start playing” (qtd. in Micconi 134–35) [1]. Demby affirmed, “Louis Armstrong and Ella Fitzgerald were happy to see a Black guy on European television… To them I was an early example of the Civil Rights revolution having transformed the racial situation in Italy” (qtd. Micconi 135).

The Italian variety show Il Signore delle 21 filmed at RAI Studios, the public Italian broadcasting company (radio and television). The company started with radio broadcasting in 1924 and added television programs in 1954 [2]. Il Signore delle 21 aired in the early 1960s and filmed at the Teatro delle Vittorie, an iconic RAI studio in Rome [3].

Cinecittà Studios

John Kitzmiller, Katherine Dunham and Ben Johnson

Cinecittà Studios gave rise to a boom known as “Hollywood on the Tiber” in the 1950s, a time in which a number of African American performers took up on screen roles, ranging from lead roles to extra work [1].

John Kitzmiller’s successful acting career in Rome began with Vivere in pace (To Live in Peace) filmed at Cinecittà Studios in 1947 [2]. Although he had a long career in Rome, he is best known in the United States for his role as Quarrel in the James Bond film Dr. No [3].

Kitzmiller’s stay in Rome was unanticipated, as he was an engineer and not an actor when offered the role in Vivere in pace by director Luigi Zampa. At the end of World War II, however, Kitzmiller had no inclination to return home to Michigan, as both his parents died during the war and his sister was moving to Detroit. Kitzmiller told his hometown paper in 1960, “At that time, thousands of discharged GIs were looking for jobs, so I figured I’d let them get settled down first” [4]. Although Kitzmiller’s on-screen performance was received positively in Italy, American critics highlighted the perpetuation of racist stereotypes of Black men, which continued through his later roles in films such as Tombolo [5]. Kitzmiller won “Best Actor” at the Cannes Film Festival in 1957 for his role in the Valley of Peace.

Though Kitzmiller was one of the leading Black actors working in Rome in this period, other luminaries included Katherine Dunham who worked in the Italian cinema in such films as Mambo and whose dance method was taught in Rome. Edith Peters, who sang with her sisters, The Peters Sisters, appeared in a number of Italian films in the 1950s and 1960s.

In and around Rome, other productions featured Black talent on screen. Ben Johnson starred in Il peccato di Anna, a retelling of Othello set in 1950s Rome, in which William Demby also played a substantive supporting role as a Black expatriate jazz musician.

Roman Bricktop’s on the Via Veneto

Bricktop

The Rome American raved, ‘Bricktop’s opening last night at the ABC Room of the Hotel Ambassador was in the old tradition—a setting jampacked with celebrities, and Bricktop herself the same warm and wonderful personality that has made her famous throughout the world’ (Bricktop, Bricktop with James Haskins 253).

After her iconic run as a performer and clubowner in interwar Paris, Ada “Bricktop” Smith opened up Roman Bricktop’s in early 1951 in the Ambassador Hotel with Charlie Lewis’s band, near Doney’s Restaurant on the Via Veneto. While it received praise and buzz, there was a decline in business as time went on. Bricktop pivoted and reopened her club in a second location in “a room behind a florist’s shop on the Via Veneto.” The building, however, was owned by priests, who “objected to the music in the small hours of the morning. They didn’t make [Bricktop] close the club, but they did stop the music, and what was Bricktop’s without music? [Bricktop’s] stayed at the florist-shop location only a few months” (Bricktop 258) [1]. This led to her final location, the one most associated with Roman Bricktop’s, which was in a cellar on the Via Veneto, a location found by her friend Jody Desmond. “I was never crazy about the place,” Bricktop recalled.” “The décor was more that of a restaurant than a nightclub. It was very chic, but it didn’t have that intimacy. A lot of my clients confessed that they didn’t like it either, but eventually we changed it around” (Bricktop 258).

Bricktop’s club was a hot spot during the period of “Hollywood on the Tiber” and was frequented by celebrities including actress Anna Magnani, Bricktop’s close friend, who gathered to hear Bricktop’s nightly performance of “Miss Otis Regrets,” penned for her by her friend Cole Porter. In Rome, Bricktop was known as the “Holy Hustler” for her twinned business savvy and staunch commitment to philanthropy, the latter which grew out of her wartime conversion to Catholicism. Bricktop ran her club in Rome until 1964, when she announced her retirement and return to the States (Bricktop 279).

Maya Angelou

When night fell I walked down the Via Veneto, past the outdoor tables of Doney’s Restaurant and into the next block where a small simple sign BRICKTOP’S hung over the door… The club had thick carpets and heavy chandeliers, and the waiters dressed as handsomely as the customers. … I looked around the room at some famous American and European faces, and at the line of people waiting inside the doorway for tables. I didn’t doubt that Brickie had the keys necessary to open the Eternal City (Angelou, Singin’ and Swingin’ and Gettin’ Merry Like Christmas 284-86).

While Maya Angelou was on tour in Paris as a dancer with Porgy and Bess, dancer Bernard Hassel, a close friend of James Baldwin’s, urged her to go to Bricktop’s nightclub if she was ever in Rome (Singin’ 284). She took his advice and was greeted warmly by Bricktop after sharing with the famed clubowner the news of the Porgy cast’s arrival to the Eternal City [2].

In Rome, Angelou received word that her son, Clyde, was ill and she would need to return to the United States as soon as possible. Without any financial support from the production, which was sponsored by the U.S. State Department, Angelou reached out to Bricktop, whom she had just met; Bricktop promptly offered Angelou moral support and a residency in her club. With earnings from performing in Porgy at Rome’s opera house, singing at Bricktop’s for two months, and teaching African dance, Angelou secured her own way back home (Singin’ 288). Angelou would return to the region soon after to live in Cairo, where she worked as a journalist before moving to Accra in the early 1960s.
